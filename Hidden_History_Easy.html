History you never learned in school, but should have.

Some things that really happened sound like they were made up. A war that lasted 38 minutes. A queen who was also a car mechanic. A plague that made people dance until they dropped. Below are 40 true stories, told in simple words. None of them are jokes. All of them are real.

— I · Time is weirder than you thinkTime plays tricks

We often picture history as "ancient people" and "modern people," with a big wall between them. But the wall is a lie. Some old things are newer than you think, and some new things are older.

01

Cleopatra lived closer to the iPhone than to the pyramids

Cleopatra was born in 69 BC. The Great Pyramid was built around 2560 BC. That is 2,500 years before her. The iPhone came out in 2007 AD, about 2,076 years after her. She was closer in time to us than to the pyramids she used to walk past.

69 BC Egypt
02

Oxford University is older than the Aztec Empire

Teaching started at Oxford around 1096. The Aztec capital, Tenochtitlan, was founded in 1325. So when the Aztecs were just beginning, students in England had already been writing essays for 200 years.

1096 Schools
03

Woolly mammoths were alive while the pyramids were being built

Most mammoths died out around 10,000 years ago. But a small group survived on Wrangel Island, near Russia, until about 1650 BC. The Great Pyramid was finished around 2560 BC. That means for about 900 years, mammoths and pyramids existed at the same time on Earth.

1650 BC Nature
04

The fax machine is older than the telephone

Alexander Bain made a working fax in 1843. That was more than 30 years before Alexander Graham Bell's telephone in 1876. For a while, you could send a picture across a wire but not hear a voice.

1843 Tech
05

France used the guillotine the same year Star Wars came out

A man named Hamida Djandoubi was executed by guillotine in Marseille on 10 September 1977. That same year, Star Wars: A New Hope opened in cinemas. Old and new, in the same summer.

1977 Law
06

Harvard University is older than calculus

Harvard was founded in 1636. Isaac Newton and Gottfried Leibniz worked out calculus in the 1660s–1680s. So for its first 30 years, Harvard taught math without calculus in the textbooks.

1636 Schools
07

The Ottoman Empire and the NBA existed at the same time

The NBA started in 1946. The Ottoman Empire officially ended in 1922. That is only 24 years of gap. Some people who watched the early Harlem Globetrotters were born when a sultan still ruled in Istanbul.

1922 / 1946 Empires

— II · Strange warsWars that sound fake

Wars are usually awful, long, and serious. These wars were still serious — but they were also short, silly, or won by animals.

08

The shortest war in history lasted 38 minutes

The Anglo‑Zanzibar War happened on 27 August 1896. Britain wanted a different sultan in power. They gave Zanzibar an ultimatum. When the time ran out, British ships fired at the palace. It was over before breakfast would finish cooking.

1896 Shortest
09

Australia lost a war to emus

In 1932, big birds called emus were eating crops in Western Australia. Soldiers with machine guns were sent to kill them. The emus ran fast, scattered in groups, and survived. The army gave up. Newspapers called it "The Great Emu War," and the emus won.

1932 Animals
10

Napoleon was defeated by a swarm of rabbits

After signing a peace treaty in 1807, Napoleon wanted a fun rabbit hunt. His staff gathered hundreds of rabbits in cages. But the rabbits had been raised by humans and were not afraid of people. When released, they ran toward Napoleon, not away. He had to run to his carriage.

1807 Oops
11

Paraguay fought Brazil, Argentina, and Uruguay at the same time

The War of the Triple Alliance (1864–1870) is one of the deadliest wars per person in modern history. Paraguay fought three bigger countries at once. It lost most of its adult men — some estimates say 60% — and has never fully recovered in population balance.

1864 South America
12

A war started over a stolen bucket

In 1325, soldiers from Modena stole a wooden bucket from a well in Bologna, Italy. The Bolognese were furious. A battle called the War of the Oaken Bucket followed. Thousands of people died. Modena kept the bucket, and it is still on display there today.

1325 Italy
13

Tug of war used to be an Olympic sport

From 1900 to 1920, tug of war was a real Olympic event. Countries sent teams of eight. Britain won gold, silver, and bronze in 1908, because they sent three police teams and no one else was as strong.

1900–1920 Sports

— III · Leaders behaving wildlyKings, queens, and chaos

Power does strange things to people. Here are leaders who made rules you would not believe if you saw them in a movie.

14

Pope Gregory IX declared war on cats

In 1233, Pope Gregory IX wrote a letter linking black cats to the devil. Across Europe, people began killing them. Some historians think this is partly why rat populations grew so fast later — helping the Black Death spread in the 1340s. A pope's bad mood may have shaped a plague.

1233 Pets
15

A king of Spain had a jaw so big he couldn't chew

Charles II of Spain (1661–1700) was the final ruler of the Spanish Habsburg family. Generations of marrying cousins left him with a huge jaw, a weak body, and many illnesses. He could not chew properly. When he died without a child, it caused the War of the Spanish Succession.

1700 Royalty
16

The dictator who renamed months after himself and his mother

Saparmurat Niyazov ruled Turkmenistan from 1990 to 2006. He renamed January "Turkmenbashi" (his title, meaning "leader of Turkmens") and renamed April "Gurbansoltan" after his mother. He also banned beards, ballet, and lip‑syncing, and wrote a holy book people had to memorize to get a driver's license.

1990s Modern
17

Queen Elizabeth II was a World War II mechanic

In 1945, 18‑year‑old Princess Elizabeth joined the Auxiliary Territorial Service. She learned to drive trucks and fix engines. She is the only British monarch in history known to have served as a trained mechanic. Later, as queen, she sometimes changed her own spark plugs.

1945 WWII
18

Einstein was offered the presidency of Israel

In 1952, after Israel's first president Chaim Weizmann died, the prime minister David Ben‑Gurion offered the job to Albert Einstein. Einstein politely said no. He wrote that he was not good with people and "lacked both the natural aptitude and the experience."

1952 Science
19

Genghis Khan has 16 million living descendants

A 2003 DNA study found that about 1 in 200 men on Earth carries a Y chromosome that traces back to Genghis Khan's family line. That is roughly 16 million people today — more than the population of many countries.

~1200 DNA
20

The CIA tried to kill Fidel Castro over 600 times

Cuba's own security chief, Fabian Escalante, once counted 638 CIA plans to kill Castro. They included exploding cigars, a poisoned wetsuit, a poisoned pen, and a plan to make his beard fall out with chemicals so he would lose respect. He survived every attempt and died naturally in 2016.

1959–2000 Cold War

— IV · Daily life, gone wrongOrdinary people, wild moments

21

A plague made 400 people dance until some died

In July 1518 in Strasbourg, a woman named Frau Troffea began to dance in the street. She did not stop for days. Soon dozens, then hundreds, joined her. Doctors told them dancing more would cure them — it did not. Some danced until they collapsed from heart attacks. The cause is still debated: stress, a fungus in bread, or mass hysteria.

1518 Mystery
22

Ancient Romans used urine as mouthwash

Urine contains ammonia, which whitens teeth. Wealthy Romans, especially in Portugal, imported bottled urine to gargle with. The government even taxed the urine trade. When people complained, Emperor Vespasian famously said, "Money does not stink."

1st century AD Gross
23

Cornflakes were invented to reduce sex drive

John Harvey Kellogg believed plain, bland food would stop people from having "impure thoughts." He created cornflakes as part of a strict diet at his wellness center in Michigan. The breakfast cereal became world famous — but not for the reason he wanted.

1894 Food
24

In the 1920s, people drank radium on purpose

Radium was new and glowed in the dark, so people thought it was healthy. "Radithor" was a drink of radium in water. Rich men drank it every day. One, a steel tycoon named Eben Byers, drank about 1,400 bottles. His jaw later fell off and he died a painful death in 1932.

1920s Health
25

Tulip bulbs once cost more than a house

In the Netherlands in 1636–1637, single tulip bulbs of rare colors sold for the price of a mansion in Amsterdam. Then the market crashed in a single week. It is one of the first recorded "bubbles" in economic history, and people still study it today.

1637 Money
26

The 1904 Olympic marathon was a total disaster

The race was run in 32°C heat on dusty roads. The first man to finish rode 17 km in a car. The winner, Thomas Hicks, was given strychnine (rat poison in small doses) and brandy as a "performance boost." A Cuban runner stopped to eat rotten apples and slept in a ditch. He still finished fourth.

1904 Sports
27

Fridges used to kill people in their sleep

Early refrigerators from the 1910s–20s leaked poisonous gases like ammonia, methyl chloride, and sulfur dioxide. Families died from the fumes while sleeping. A team including Einstein tried to design a safer fridge — which is why "Einstein refrigerator" is a real thing.

1926 Home

— V · Science and tech, but weirderScience in strange places

28

The US seriously planned to nuke the Moon

Project A119 (1958) was a secret US Air Force plan to explode a nuclear bomb on the Moon. The goal was to scare the Soviet Union during the space race. A young Carl Sagan worked on the math. The plan was cancelled because the public would have hated it, and it might mess up future science on the Moon.

1958 Cold War
29

Roman concrete is stronger than modern concrete

Ancient Roman harbor walls have been standing in seawater for 2,000 years and are getting stronger. Scientists in 2023 finally figured out why: tiny chunks of lime in the mix react with water to self‑heal cracks. We are now trying to copy it.

~100 AD Build
30

The first computer programmer was a woman in the 1840s

Ada Lovelace, the daughter of poet Lord Byron, wrote the first algorithm meant to be run on a machine — Charles Babbage's "Analytical Engine" — in 1843. The machine was never built in her lifetime, but her code would have worked. A modern programming language is named "Ada" in her honor.

1843 Code
31

Cats domesticated themselves

Unlike dogs, humans did not really tame cats. DNA studies suggest that when humans started storing grain about 10,000 years ago in the Middle East, wild cats came close because of the mice. Friendly cats got treats. Over thousands of years, they became our pets without us planning it.

~8000 BC Animals
32

The word "robot" is only about 100 years old

The word comes from a 1920 play by Czech writer Karel Čapek called R.U.R. ("Rossum's Universal Robots"). It is based on the Czech word "robota," meaning forced labor. Before 1920, there were machines — but no "robots."

1920 Language
33

A cow called Elsie helped the Manhattan Project

During WWII, scientists needed safe materials that would not react with uranium. A research cow named Elsie at Oak Ridge helped test how radiation moved through living bodies. Many animals quietly played small roles in making the atomic bomb possible.

1944 WWII

— VI · Myths we still believe"Everyone knows" — but it's wrong

Some "facts" get repeated so often that no one checks them. Here are popular history stories that are actually false or badly wrong.

34

The Great Wall of China is NOT visible from space

This myth began in a 1932 comic book. Astronauts, including Chinese astronaut Yang Liwei in 2003, have said they cannot see it with the naked eye from low orbit. It is long but very thin — only about 5–9 meters wide.

Myth Space
35

Vikings did NOT wear horned helmets

Real Viking helmets were plain metal or leather caps. The horned helmet image comes from a 19th‑century opera costume designed by Carl Emil Doepler for Wagner's "Ring" cycle in 1876. Horns would be a terrible idea in a real fight — easy to grab.

Myth Vikings
36

Napoleon was not short for his time

Napoleon Bonaparte was about 1.68 m (5'6"). That was average for a Frenchman in 1800. The myth of his shortness came from British cartoons and a mix‑up between French and British inches. Today, people still say "Napoleon complex" because of a joke that was wrong.

Myth Size
37

Marie Antoinette never said "Let them eat cake"

The quote appeared in Rousseau's writing in 1767, when Marie Antoinette was still a child in Austria. It became linked to her later to make the French queen look cruel before the revolution. Real history: there is no evidence she ever said it.

Myth France
38

Columbus did NOT prove the Earth was round

Educated Europeans in 1492 already knew the Earth was round. Greeks had proved it around 240 BC — Eratosthenes even measured its size with shadows. The fight against Columbus was about distance, not shape: experts correctly said Asia was too far, and Columbus was just lucky America was in the way.

Myth Explore
39

Witches at Salem were not burned

In the 1692 Salem trials, 19 people were hanged, one was pressed with heavy stones, and several died in jail. None were burned at the stake. The "burning witches" image comes from Europe, mainly Germany, a century earlier.

1692 USA
40

And one that's true but sounds fake: Pepsi once had the 6th largest navy

In 1989, the Soviet Union was short of cash but wanted Pepsi for its citizens. In a barter deal, the USSR paid with 17 old submarines, a cruiser, a frigate, and a destroyer. For a brief time, Pepsi technically owned the 6th largest submarine fleet in the world. The CEO joked to the White House that his company was "disarming the Soviets faster than you are." The ships were quickly sold for scrap — Pepsi was a drink company, not a navy.

1989 Cola Cold War

— codaWhy these stories matter

The past was not serious, tidy, and black‑and‑white. It was chaotic, funny, tragic, and full of humans making very human choices.
One idea to take with you: when something in history sounds "too weird to be true," don't rule it out. Real life has always been stranger than our textbooks tell us. The next time someone says "back then, people were simple," remember the dancing plague, the emu war, and the pope who declared war on cats.
