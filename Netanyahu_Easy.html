Chapter One

A Boy in Two Worlds

He was born in Tel Aviv. He went to high school in Philadelphia. He learned to live with one foot in Israel and one foot in America.

Benjamin Netanyahu was born on October 21, 1949. Israel was only one year old. His family called him "Bibi," and people still call him that today.

His father, Benzion, was a history teacher. He studied the history of the Jewish people. Benzion had strong ideas. He believed that enemies of Jewish people would never stop, so Jewish people must always be strong. These ideas stuck in Bibi's head for life.

When Bibi was a teenager, his family moved to the United States because his father got a job there. Bibi finished high school near Philadelphia. He spoke perfect English, with an American sound to it. That small thing would matter a lot later — because American TV and American leaders would always feel comfortable with him.

Chapter Two

The Army Years

At 18, he flew back to Israel to join the army. He did not go into a normal unit. He joined one of the most famous fighting groups in the country.

The unit is called Sayeret Matkal. It is a small, secret group of soldiers who do very hard jobs — rescuing hostages, hunting terrorists, sneaking behind enemy lines. You have to be smart and tough to get in.

Bibi served for five years. He fought in real battles. He was wounded in the shoulder in a rescue of a hijacked plane in 1972. His brother Yoni was a soldier in the same unit. So was his brother Iddo. Three brothers, all commandos.

After the army, Bibi went back to America for college. He studied at MIT, a famous school for science and engineering in Boston. He got two degrees there — in building design and in business.

Good to know

Many Israeli leaders start their stories in the army. For Bibi, the army years made him tough, gave him a group of loyal friends, and showed him how to act under pressure.

Chapter Three

A Brother Named Yoni

On July 4, 1976, something happened that changed Bibi's life forever. His older brother died a hero.

A French plane had been hijacked by terrorists. The plane landed in Uganda, in Africa, far from Israel. The terrorists wanted money and Palestinian prisoners to be set free. They held over 100 Jewish and Israeli people on the plane.

Israel decided to do something wild. They sent soldiers on planes, in secret, all the way to Africa. The soldiers landed at the airport at night, fought the terrorists, and saved the hostages. It was one of the most famous rescue missions in history.

The leader of that rescue was Yonatan Netanyahu — Bibi's older brother, everyone called him Yoni. He was the only Israeli soldier killed in the raid. Just one bullet.

"Better to die on your feet than to live on your knees." — Words often used by the Netanyahu family

Yoni became a national hero. Books were written about him. His young brother Bibi, who was now 26, felt he had to carry on Yoni's fight. From that day on, Bibi's public life was about one big idea: terrorists must never win, and Israel must never be weak.

Chapter Four

First Steps in Politics

He wasn't a politician yet. But he was smart, good on camera, and he knew how to speak to Americans.

After his brother died, Bibi started a group to study terrorism. He wrote books about it. He gave many interviews on American TV. People saw a young man in a nice suit, with clear English and strong ideas. He looked like a politician before he was one.

In 1982, Israel sent him to the United States as a deputy ambassador. Four years later, he became Israel's ambassador to the United Nations in New York. There, he made Israel's case in front of the whole world. During the Gulf War in 1991, when Iraqi missiles were falling on Tel Aviv, his calm TV interviews made him a star.

In 1988, he came home and ran for a seat in the Israeli parliament. He won. In 1993, he became the leader of the Likud party — the biggest right-wing party in Israel. He was only 43 years old.

Chapter Five

Prime Minister at Last

In 1996, at age 46, he became the youngest prime minister Israel had ever had. But the job was hard, and it did not last long.

Before he won, a big thing happened: the peace-making prime minister, Yitzhak Rabin, was killed by a Jewish man who hated the peace deals with the Palestinians. The country was shocked and sad. A few months later, Israelis voted for a new leader.

Netanyahu ran against Shimon Peres, the old man of Israeli politics. Almost no one thought Bibi would win. But there were bus bombings in Israel, and people were scared. They picked the tough man instead of the dove. Bibi won by less than one percent.

His first time as prime minister was rough. He kept fighting with his own team. He kept fighting with the Americans. He slowed down the peace deals but could not stop them. In 1999, he lost the next election to a soldier named Ehud Barak, and went home.

Did you know?

Bibi was the first Israeli prime minister born after the country was created in 1948. The older leaders had all been part of the founding of Israel. Bibi was a new kind of leader — a TV-age politician.

Chapter Six

Out, and Back Again

Most politicians who lose don't come back. Bibi did. It took him ten years, but he did it.

Out of the prime minister's office, Bibi did not quit. He became finance minister in the 2000s and cut lots of spending to help the economy grow. The poor did not love him for it. But the economy became stronger.

Slowly, he built himself back up. He studied his old mistakes. He became more careful. In 2009, his Likud party won the election, and Bibi was prime minister again. This time, he stayed — for twelve long years.

Chapter Seven

The Long Run

From 2009 to 2021, Bibi was prime minister. Twelve years in a row. No one had ever done that in Israel before.

During this long time, Bibi did many things. He kept the economy strong. He built up Israel's tech industry. He made friends with many world leaders. He was very worried about one country above all: Iran.

Netanyahu said Iran wanted to build nuclear bombs and destroy Israel. He worked hard to stop that. In 2015, he even flew to America and spoke to the U.S. Congress to try to stop a deal that President Obama was making with Iran. That was not normal — and it made some people in America angry. But Bibi did not care. He felt he was fighting for Israel's life.

Inside Israel, he also slowed down any real talks with the Palestinians. He said peace was a nice idea but not possible now. Many Israelis agreed with him, especially after many years of bombs and attacks.

"If the Arabs put down their weapons, there will be no more war. If Israel puts down its weapons, there will be no more Israel." — A line Netanyahu often uses

Chapter Eight

Money, Gifts, and Court

In 2019, something happened that no other prime minister had ever had to deal with. Bibi was charged with crimes.

The police had been watching him for years. They said he and his wife, Sara, had taken a lot of free gifts — champagne, fancy cigars, jewelry — from rich friends, and in return had done favors for them. In another case, they said he had tried to get nicer news stories written about him by giving a newspaper a better deal.

Netanyahu said no, it was all lies. He said the police, the courts, and the news were all out to get him. His fans believed him. Others thought the evidence was strong.

The trial started in 2020. As of 2026, it is still going on. Trials in Israel take a very long time. Bibi has never been found guilty. But the shadow of these cases has followed him every day.

Chapter Nine

A New Deal with Arab Neighbors

In 2020, something surprising happened. Israel made peace with some Arab countries that had not been friends before.

With the help of U.S. President Donald Trump, Israel signed deals with the United Arab Emirates, Bahrain, Sudan, and Morocco. These were called the Abraham Accords, named after a man in the Bible who is seen as the father of both Jewish people and Arab people.

For Bibi, this was a huge win. For many years, the rule was: no peace with Arabs until the Palestinian problem is fixed. Now, he had broken that rule. Israel was making friends without Palestinians at the table.

Many Palestinians were very upset. They felt left out and forgotten. But Israelis who supported Bibi loved him for this — a real, new peace, without giving up any land.

Why it matters

The Abraham Accords changed the Middle East. For the first time in a long time, Israel had normal, open friendships with Arab countries on the other side of the region.

Chapter Ten

October 7, 2023

Netanyahu was back as prime minister. Then, the worst day in Israel's modern history came on his watch.

In 2021, Bibi had lost power for about a year and a half to a group of other parties who worked together to push him out. But their group was shaky. By the end of 2022, he was prime minister again, with the most right-wing government Israel had ever had.

Then came October 7, 2023. On that morning, a Jewish holiday, fighters from Hamas broke through the fence around Gaza. They killed about 1,200 people in Israel — families, children, people at a music festival. They took about 250 hostages back to Gaza. It was the darkest day for Jewish people since the Holocaust.

Many Israelis blamed Bibi. They asked: how did this happen on your watch? Why did the army not know? Why did the government ignore the warnings? He had always said he was the safe choice, the strong leader. Now the country had never felt more broken.

"There will be a time to ask the hard questions. That time is not now." — Netanyahu, after October 7

Even so, he did not resign. He said Israel needed a wartime leader, and that leader was him.

Chapter Eleven

War, and More War

The war that started on October 7 did not stay in one place. It grew, and grew, and spread.

Israel's army went into Gaza. Bombs from the sky, tanks on the ground. The fighting was very bloody. Tens of thousands of Palestinians were killed. Most of Gaza was damaged. Food, water, and homes were hard to find. Much of the world watched with horror.

Then the fighting spread. Hezbollah, a group in Lebanon, fired rockets at Israel. Israel fired back, and later sent soldiers into Lebanon. The Houthis, a group in Yemen, shot at ships in the Red Sea. In 2024, for the first time ever, Israel and Iran fired missiles at each other directly. It was the biggest regional war in a long time.

Through all of this, Netanyahu kept fighting. Some hostages came home — a few through deals, a few through army raids. Others are still in Gaza. In late 2024, a huge shock: the family of Bashar al-Assad lost power in Syria, after more than 50 years.

Chapter Twelve

Netanyahu in 2026

He is 76 years old. He has been prime minister for more than 17 years in total. And he is running again.

In April 2026, Bibi is still Israel's prime minister. He was re-elected as the leader of his Likud party. He has said he will run in the next Israeli election. Many people cheer him. Many people want him gone. Both groups are big, and neither is going away.

His court case is still going on. The war has cooled down in some places and heated up in others. Israel is tired but not broken. The Palestinian question is still unanswered. Peace feels very far, but not impossible.

The man behind the politics

Bibi has a wife, Sara, and three children — Yair, Avner, and Noa. He loves old movies. He smokes cigars. He writes his own speeches. He works late into the night. His friends say he is warm in private and stiff in public.

His fans call him the savior of Israel. His critics call him the person most responsible for how divided Israel has become. Both could be right at the same time.

The long view

No leader of a small country has shaped its recent story more than Netanyahu has shaped Israel's. Whether he stays in power one more year or ten, his name will be in the history books forever.
